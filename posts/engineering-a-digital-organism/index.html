Engineering A Digital Organism

USER: SYS_ADMIN | DATE: 2026-03-30 | REF_PID: Connectome-Fruit-Fly-Blog

The Virtual World

Taking a static map of 50 million synapses and turning it into a living simulation requires serious computational heavy lifting. In 2026, a team at Eon Systems took the FlyWire connectome and built a computational bridge between the biological map and a digital physics engine. To pull this off, they had to solve three massive engineering problems:

  • Simulating the brain
  • Building the physical body
  • Creating a real-time control loop to sync them together

1. Simulating the Brain: The LIF Model

Instead of trying to simulate every complex biophysical molecule and protein channel inside a fly’s head, the researchers translated the static connectome network into a Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) model (based heavily on work by Shiu et al., 2024)
You can think of LIF digital neurons as tiny, leaky capacitors. Here is how they operate:

  • Integrate: As a neuron receives excitatory signals from its upstream connections, it builds up “voltage.”
  • Leak: Over time, if no new signals arrive, that voltage naturally drains away
  • Fire: If the incoming signals happen fast enough to overcome the leak and push the voltage past a specific threshold, the digital neuron “fires” a binary spike to its downstream neighbors and immediately resets to its baseline

Because they had the exact neurotransmitter identities from the FlyWire project (knowing which synapses were excitatory and which were inhibitory), they could accurately model how signals cascade through the 140,000-node network.

2. Building the Body: NeuroMechFly & MuJoCo

A brain in a void does nothing; it needs an embodied form to experience a world. To give the brain a body, the team utilized NeuroMechFly (specifically v2, developed by Wang-Chen et al., 2024).

This isn’t just a basic 3D video game asset. NeuroMechFly is a highly precise, biomechanical 3D mesh generated from an actual X-ray microtomography scan of a biological fruit fly. It features 87 independent, physically simulated joints.

This complex anatomical model is deployed inside MuJoCo (Multi-Joint dynamics with Contact), a highly advanced physics engine originally developed by Emo Todorov. MuJoCo is the gold standard in robotics and biomechanics because it is uniquely optimized to calculate continuous, rigid-body dynamics and complex contact forces, like the microscopic friction and torque of six tiny fly legs interacting with the ground.

3. The Interface: The 15ms Control Loop

The real magic is the control loop the bridge between the MuJoCo physics engine (the world/body) and the LIF network (the brain). The system runs a synchronization loop every 15 milliseconds:

  1. Sensory Input (World to Brain): The physics engine detects a physical interaction—like virtual “dust” hitting the antennae mesh or a leg colliding with a virtual sugar droplet. This physical trigger is translated into electrical spikes that artificially stimulate the corresponding sensory neurons (mechanosensors or gustatory receptors) in the LIF brain model)
  2. Neural Processing: The LIF model computes the resulting cascade of electrical activity across the 50 million synapses for that 15ms window, governed entirely by the connectome’s structural logic
  3. Motor Output (Brain to World): The system reads the output of the “descending neurons” the specific neurons that carry commands from the brain to the body. For example, if the brain fires the DNa01 or DNa02 neurons, it signals a steering command. If it fires the MN9 motor neurons, it commands the proboscis to move
  4. Actuation: These low-dimensional neural commands are translated into physical joint torques in MuJoCo, causing the virtual fly to actually move its digital muscles
